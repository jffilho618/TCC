ABSTRACT
Objective: Research on auxiliary diagnosis of primary bone tumors can enhance diagnostic accuracy, facilitate 
early detection, and enable personalized treatment, thereby reducing misdiagnosis and missed cases, ultimately 
leading to improved patient prognosis and survival rates. In this study, we established a whole slide imaging 
(WSI) database comprising histopathological samples from all categories of bone tumors and integrated multiple 
neural network architectures for machine learning models. We then evaluated the accuracy of these models in 
diagnosing primary bone tumors.
Methods: In this paper, the machine learning model based on the deep convolutional neural network (DC-NN) 
method was combined with imaging omics analysis to analyze and discuss its clinical value in diagnosing pri -
mary bone tumors. In addition, this paper proposed a screening method for differentially expressed genes. Based 
on the paired T-test method, the process first estimated the tumor purity in the experimental data of each sample 
case, then assessed the actual gene expression value of the experimental data of each sample case, and finally 
calculated the optimized paired T-test statistics, and screened differentially expressed genes according to the 
threshold value.
Results: The selected model demonstrated excellent diagnostic accuracy in distinguishing between normal and 
tumor images, with overall accuracy of (99.8 ± 0.4) % for five rounds of testing using the DCNN model and 
positive and negative predictive values of (100.0 ± 0.0) % and (99.6 ± 0.8) %, respectively. The mean area 
under each dataset ’ s curve (AUC) was (0.998 ± 0.004). Further, ten rounds of testing using the DCNN model 
showed an overall accuracy of (71.2 ± 1.6) % and a substantial positive predictive value of (91.9 ± 8.5) % in 
distinguishing benign from malignant bone tumors, with an average AUC of (0.62 ± 0.06) across datasets.
Conclusion: The deep learning model accurately classifies bone tumor histopathology based on the degree of 
infiltration, achieving diagnostic performance comparable to that of senior pathologists. These findings affirm 
the feasibility and effectiveness of histopathological diagnosis in bone tumors, providing a theoretical foundation 
for the application and advancement of machine learning-assisted histopathological diagnosis in this field.

1. Introduction
In recent years, cancer has posed increasingly severe challenges to 
human health. With the accumulation of medical data and breakthrough 
developments in artificial intelligence technology, efficiently assisting in 
tumor diagnosis has become challenging for bioinformatics and com -
puter disciplines. Primary bone tumors constitute only 0.2 % of human 
tumors, with an annual incidence of approximately 20 cases per million 
population [ 1 ]. These tumors often inflict damage to bone structure, 
leading to long-term dysfunction and diminished quality of life [ 2,3 ]. 
Currently, multimodal therapy, incorporating adjuvant chemotherapy 
and limb salvage surgery, is regarded as the standard treatment for most 
respectable primary bone tumors like osteosarcoma and Ewing ’ s sar -
coma. Adjuvant chemotherapy and targeted therapy also offer thera -
peutic benefits for some instances of unresectable tumors [ 4,5 ]. Early 
detection and diagnosis of malignant bone tumors are crucial to prevent 
distant metastasis and enhance patients ’ limb preservation rate, survival 
rate, and quality of life. X-ray imaging remains the most commonly 
utilized imaging tool for diagnosing primary bone tumors [ 6,7 ].
However, due to the low incidence of primary bone tumors, the 
complexity of histological subtypes, and the diversity of imaging fea -
tures, the accuracy of plain film diagnosis is limited, and newer but more 
expensive techniques such as CT and MRI are usually needed to confirm 
suspected lesions seen on plain film [8] . The limited knowledge of pri -
mary doctors in X-ray image interpretation of bone tumors is a common 
factor causing misdiagnosis and delayed diagnosis of bone tumors [9] . 
Improving the diagnostic accuracy of screening X-ray images is crucial 
for early detection or exclusion of primary bone tumors. Diagnosing 
bone tumors is the first step in the medical management of patients, and 
it is of great value to guide the treatment plan. Early and correct diag -
nosis can provide timely intervention for lesions, which not only eases 
the progression of the disease and reduces the probability of tumor 
spread and metastasis but also improves the overall survival rate of 
patients and reduces the physical and mental pain and economic burden 
of patients [9] . Missed diagnosis or misdiagnosis may cause patients to 
miss the best treatment opportunity, which seriously affects the quality 
of life and prognosis [10] .
The diagnostic criteria for bone tumors include: 
1) Epidemiological characteristics, such as age, sex, and location of the 
disease;
2) Symptoms: including pain, swelling, pathological fractures, etc;
3) Signs such as tenderness, vein irritation, and elevated skin 
temperature;
4) Imaging examinations: identifying features such as lesion site, 
osteolytic transformation, invasive growth, periosteal reaction, etc., 
with specific findings such as a hardened area in CT scans, soft tissue 
infiltration, and high signal intensity in MRI scans, and increased 
radioactive uptake in radionuclide bone imaging;
5) Gross morphology of the tumor: considering its shape, color, texture, 
size, etc;
6) Assistive technologies, including immunohistochemistry, electron 
microscope scanning, special staining, flow cytometry, karyotype 
analysis, and protein/gene detection.
However, traditional diagnostic methods such as medical history and 
imaging have limitations. For example, while epidemiological indicators 
can effectively identify individual tumors, most bone tumors tend to 
have a trend of onset at a young age, a preference for males, and diverse 
incidence sites. Similarly, symptoms and signs of tumors are often 
similar and nonspecific, with most benign tumors displaying hidden 
growth and most malignant tumors causing pain and swelling. Although 
imaging evaluation may identify specific features such as the “ Codman 
triangle ” indicating osteosarcoma or cartilage cap protrusion indicating 
osteochondroma, many tumors, particularly those with a low incidence 
of malignancy, lack typical imaging features, making reliance on 
imaging alone for diagnosis unreliable [ 19,20 ].
Histopathological diagnosis remains the gold standard for deter -
mining the biological nature of tumors. In clinical practice, histopath -
ological examination involves lesion biopsy and qualitative tumor 
diagnosis conducted through multidisciplinary collaboration among 
orthopedic, imaging, and pathology physicians [ 21 ]. Therefore, accu -
rate and reliable histopathological diagnosis is crucial for determining 
the appropriate treatment plan for bone tumors.
Artificial intelligence, such as machine learning, in the medical field 
is increasing rapidly [22] . Some recent image omics feature analyses can 
more effectively and accurately diagnose diseases, predict prognosis, 
and evaluate the potential efficacy of targeted therapy through DCNN 
[12] . For example, Olczak et al. [13] used five publicly available deep- 
learning networks as a diagnostic tool. They accurately diagnosed 83 % 
of fractures, comparable to the accuracy of experienced orthopedic 
surgeons. Fave et al. [14] designed a predictive model using imaging 
omics analysis. They demonstrated that CT image texture intensity 
measured at the end of radiotherapy could effectively distinguish be -
tween high and low-risk patients with stage III non-small cell lung 
cancer. In this study, we developed a machine learning model to eval -
uate its performance in diagnosing primary bone tumors around the 
knee joint. As reported below, we analyzed its ability to distinguish 
benign from malignant bone tumors.
2. Data and methods
2.1. General information
The clinical data of patients with primary bone tumors at distal 
femur, proximal tibia, or proximal fibula admitted to our hospital from 
March 2017 to December 2023 were retrospectively analyzed. Two 
experienced pathologists evaluated the biopsy or excised specimen and 
provided a histological tumor diagnosis. This study examined 50 pa -
tients with malignant tumors and 25 patients with benign tumors, and 
the orthography of various lesions was used before treatment. In addi -
tion, 75 fully confirmed orthographies of normal knee joints were 
extracted from the imaging system of our hospital as normal controls, 
and there was no difference in gender between the three groups of 
subjects (P > 0.05), as shown in Table 1 .
This study extracted the image in DICOM format and then converted 
it to JPEG format for further analysis.
2.2. Influence pretreatment and experimental design
Firstly, the data set was preprocessed, and 130 X-ray images were 
divided into the training and test sets. Because of the different brightness 
of the images, the X-ray images are converted into gray images before 
extracting the features. DCNN has achieved great success in image 
classification in many fields [15 – 17] . Compared with support vector 
machines, DCNN can automatically extract image features [18] . This 
study used a 34-layer depth residual network (ResNet34) to automati -
cally extract and classify multiple features from X-ray images [19] . The 
advantage of ResNet is the use of residual blocks to solve the problem of 
network degradation caused by increasing network depth. The features 
extracted from the shallow layer are transmitted to the deep layer 
through the residual block, as shown in Fig. 1 .
2.3. Statistical method
SPSS22.0 was used for statistical analysis of the obtained data. 
Measurement data were expressed with ( x ± s) and compared with the t - 
test. Statistical data were expressed as rates. The comparison was per -
formed using two tests. Analysis of variance was used for multi-group 
comparison. ROC curve was used to explore the clinical value of 
DCNN in diagnosing primary bone tumors around the knee joint.

3. Results
3.1. Representative X-ray images of the knee joint and radiographs of 
benign and malignant tumors
130 positive images of the knee, including 130 “ normal images ” 
without bone tumors and 130 “ tumor images ” with bone tumors, were 
randomly divided into five groups. Each group comprised 52 standard 
images ( Fig. 2 a) and 52 tumor images. Each group of 52 tumor images 
included 36 malignant ( Fig. 2 b-d) and 16 benign ( Fig. 2 e-g) tumor 
images.
3.2. DCNN model diagnosed bone tumors
Five rounds of tests were conducted using the DCNN model to 
evaluate the model ’ s ability to differentiate between “ normal images ” 
and “ tumor images ” to test its efficacy in bone tumor diagnosis. While 
each group is used as the test set, the other four groups are used as the 
training set. Finally, five different diagnostic accuracy values were ob -
tained. The DCNN classifier is highly accurate, with four out of five tests 
showing 100 % accuracy in bone tumor diagnosis. Only one standard 
image from one round of tests was misdiagnosed. The overall accuracy 
was (99.8 ± 0.3) %, while the positive predictive value and negative 
predictive value were (100.0 ± 0.0) % and (99.7 ± 0.5) %, respectively, 
as shown in Table 2 . In addition to accuracy, this study also uses AUC as 
a performance measure for machine learning. The AUC of each dataset

dence interval, 1.000 – 1.000), 1.00 (95 % confidence interval,

3.3. DCNN model distinguished benign and malignant tumors
This study evaluated the ability of DCNN to distinguish between 
benign and malignant tumors. Each group included 36 images of ma -
lignant and 16 of benign tumors. In each test round, 3 of the five groups 
are assigned as the training data set, while the other two groups are 
assigned as the test data set. 10 rounds (C25 = 10) of testing were 
conducted using the DCNN model, and the results are shown in Table 3 . 
The overall accuracy was (71.2 ± 1.6) %, achieving a substantial posi -
tive predictive value (91.9 ± 8.5 %) in distinguishing benign from ma -
lignant bone tumors, and the mean AUC was (0.62 ± 0.06), as shown in 
Table 3 .
3.4. Validation of simulation algorithm based on matching characteristics 
of tumor purity information
To evaluate the performance of the paired feature selection method 
proposed in this chapter in screening differentially expressed genes, four 
commonly used paired feature selection methods were selected in the 
experiment for comparison with the method used in this paper, namely, 
the paired T-test method (P-test) [5] and the paired T-test method based 
on multiple changes proposed by Cao et al. [9] , write it as C-test; The 
modified version of the paired T-test method proposed by Tan et al. [17]
is denoted as T-test. The combined method of Tanetal and Caoetal [12]
is denoted as the TC test. The technique used in this article is labeled 
Test.
The main design idea of the experiment is as follows: First, the actual 
differentially expressed genes (i.e., tDEG genes) are determined, the 
number of tDEG genes is known when constructing the simulation data 
set, and the tDEG genes are sorted in descending order according to the 
Δ value of genes; Then, the predicted differentially expressed genes (i.e., 
pDEG genes) were calculated, and pDEG genes were screened out in the 
simulated data set using the paired feature selection method, and sorted 
in ascending order according to the p-value of the hypothesis test. 
Finally, according to the two sorted tDEG gene lists and pDEG gene lists, 
the first m genes were selected each time to calculate the screening 
accuracy and recall rate, where m values ranged from 1 to N to draw a P- 
R curve. When the value of m is more significant than M, there is no 
differential expression of genes in the simulated data set, and the P-R 
curve remains unchanged. Therefore, only 10 % of the total number of 
genes are considered in the P-R curve drawing (in the simulation 
experiment, M is set to 10 % of N). This is also why the highest recall rate 
on the P-R curve in the experimental results is only 0.1. Figure A-D is a 
comparison of P-R curves of Scenarios1, 2, 3, and 4 SCENARIos1, 
respectively, in which the X-axis indicates the recall rate, the Y-axis 
indicates the accuracy, and different colors of curves reflect different 
experimental results of the matching feature selection methods. Since 
only 10 % of the total number of genes are considered in the calculation 
of P-R curves, these are partial, with a maximum recall rate of 0.1.
Four commonly used paired feature selection methods were 
compared on four sets of simulation data, and the experimental results 
are shown in Fig. 3 . Experimental results show that the method in this 
chapter has the maximum P-R area under the curve and is stable in four 
simulation data sets, and has strong robustness. According to the results, 
24, 49, and 74 tDEG genes are predicted among the first 25, 50, and 75 
pDEG genes in the Scenarios1 dataset, respectively, and the accuracy 
rates of SCENARIOS1 dataset are 96 %, 98 %, and 98.7 %, respectively,

4. Discuss
Bone tumor is a disease with high incidence in adolescents. Malig -
nant bone tumors often have a high mortality; early detection and early 
treatment are the key points to treat bone tumors. However, due to the 
limitations of medical treatment, many patients cannot be diagnosed in 
time, thus missing the best opportunity to treat bone tumors. In this 
paper, machine learning is used to study the diagnosis of bone tumors, 
and two algorithms are mainly proposed. The first is a model training 
algorithm based on sample information measurement, and the second is 
a bone tumor diagnosis algorithm based on multi-feature fusion.
This study showcased the high accuracy of a newly developed 
machine-learning model in diagnosing bone tumors by analyzing image- 
omics features. Subsequently, it assessed the model ’ s efficacy in differ -
entiating between benign and malignant bone tumors. Most primary 
care facilities can readily and affordably provide X-ray images, which 
hold significant value in screening for bone lesions. There has been 
debate regarding the number of images necessary for imaging omics 
research. Previous studies have demonstrated that moderate diagnostic 
accuracy or predictive value can be achieved through machine learning 
using tens or even hundreds of CT or MR images. However, more robust 
results are anticipated with an expanded sample size, reaching thou -
sands or even tens of thousands of images. A smaller sample size can be 
acceptable, particularly for rare diseases like primary bone tumors, if the 
detection accuracy is high. Despite the inclusion of only 130 images in 
this study, the DCNN model achieved remarkable accuracy ( > 99 %) in 
distinguishing bone tumor images from standard X-ray images.
The model did not demonstrate the ability to accurately distinguish 
between benign and malignant bone tumors in the 130 images studied, 
which may be due to significant imaging differences within the benign 
or malignant bone tumor groups. However, the DCNN method can 
produce a high positive predictive value, which indicates that the 
probability of missing malignant lesions during screening is low, and 
further tests such as CT, MR, And biopsy can be guided accordingly. In 
this way, the method can guide clinical decision management.
AUC is a crucial indicator for evaluating the efficacy of machine 
learning models, but due to different goals, this indicator varies signif -
icantly among various clinical studies [24 – 26] . Regarding diagnostic 
use, the best AUC results should be superior to or equal to those of ex -
perts in the field of study. Otherwise, they may not be suitable for 
clinical use. An AUC slightly below the expert level may be acceptable 
when screening a large number of images, which may reduce the labor 
intensity of the imageologist. In predictive use, a minimum AUC of about 
60 – 70 % is required to justify the medium predictive value of a machine 
learning model [27] . In this study, although the DCNN model achieved 
perfect accuracy in diagnosing bone tumors based on X-ray images, this 
model had a low negative predictive value in distinguishing benign from 
malignant bone tumors. The machine learning model may need help to 
complete the task because only a few dozen objects are included in each 
training set. Accuracy may be higher if more images are added and 
included in the training set.
The predictive value that defines whether a test has clinical appli -
cation value varies in different clinical situations. Regarding comparing 
standard and bone tumor images, doctors need high positive and 
negative predictive values; only in this way can the radiologist not miss 
any patients with tumors and not cause too many unnecessary further 
tests [28 – 30] . The ideal detection characteristics may be more nuanced 
when comparing benign and malignant tumors, depending on how im -
aging results guide clinical practice. For instance, a bone oncologist 
conducting a biopsy for suspected malignancies aims to avoid missing 
any malignancies. In such cases, the oncologist prioritizes a model with a 
high positive predictive value, prioritizing sensitivity over specificity. 
Therefore, when assessing the suitability of a model for clinical use, 
overall accuracy is not the sole factor physicians consider.
This paper still needs to explore some problems in studying bone 
tumors. The following two points are mainly important: the first is that 
the accuracy of benign and malignant bone tumor detection needs to be 
improved; the second is that the information measurement algorithm 
can be further optimized and adapted. At present, the identification of 
bone tumors has achieved a very high accuracy rate, and the identifi -
cation accuracy rate of benign and malignant is also of practical sig -
nificance for diagnosing actual conditions. In addition to the problem of 
insufficient sample size, there is also the problem of a finer division of 
bone tumor types that needs to be addressed. As there are dozens of 
benign and malignant bone tumors, more in-depth studies may be 
required on different types of bone tumors to solve the problem of low 
accuracy in classifying benign and malignant bone tumors. Such 
research requires a large sample size and the assistance of professional 
doctors.
For model training algorithms, there may be more efficient infor -
mation measurement methods in sample selection, which requires more 
in-depth research on the distribution of data sets, or a measurement 
method that can automatically adapt to different data sets of informa -
tion can be found [31] . The method proposed in this paper needs to 
adjust the weights of average information entropy and standard devia -
tion. In the future, the network may need to automatically adjust the 
weights to achieve a more efficient and accurate model training 
algorithm.

validation, where the images were evenly divided into five sets. Each 
dataset served as a test set in turn, while the remaining four sets were 
combined as a training set for each computational cycle. The bone tumor 
images were evenly distributed into five sets for both the benign and 
malignant models. Two of the five sets were chosen as the test set, while 
the remaining three were merged as the training set. Although intricate, 
this method allows for the application of imaging omics analysis in 
studying rare diseases, such as primary bone tumors, even with limited 
sample sizes. It effectively mitigates discrepancies between dataset 
groups and enhances test reproducibility. This approach to grouping 
may aid in constructing machine learning models for rare diseases, 
where collecting sufficient samples for study may take a considerable 
amount of time.
In summary, using machine learning, the image omics analysis of X- 
ray images is an effective and valuable method for bone tumor diag -
nosis. The DCNN method has not demonstrated the ability to distinguish 
between benign and malignant tumors accurately, but its high positive 
predictive value contributes to its clinical application. In future work, 
we will conduct prospective randomized controlled trials to evaluate the 
clinical application of machine learning models in the early diagnosis of 
primary healthcare bone tumors.
