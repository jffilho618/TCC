Primary malignant bone tumors are the third highest cause of cancer-related mortality among patients 
under the age of 20. X-ray scan is the primary tool for detecting bone tumors. However, due to the 
varying morphologies of bone tumors, it is challenging for radiologists to make a definitive diagnosis 
based on radiographs. With the recent advancement in deep learning algorithms, there is a surge of 
interest in computer-aided diagnosis of primary bone tumors. Nonetheless, the development in this 
field has been hindered by the lack of publicly available X-ray datasets for bone tumors. To tackle this 
challenge, we established the Bone Tumor X-ray Radiograph dataset (termed BTXRD) in collaboration 
with multiple medical institutes and hospitals. The BTXRD dataset comprises 3,746 bone images (1,879 
normal and 1,867 tumor), with clinical information and global labels available for each image, and 
distinct mask and annotated bounding box for each tumor instance. This publicly available dataset can 
support the development and evaluation of deep learning algorithms for the diagnosis of primary bone 
tumors.
Background & Summary
Recent advances in Artificial Intelligence (AI), especially deep learning, have provided transformative possi -
bilities in computer-aided diagnosis systems. Deep learning-based techniques, such as segmentation, detec -
tion, classification, and enhancement, have been successfully applied to medical imaging 1–3. Existing studies 
demonstrate the potential of these techniques in identifying and analyzing bone tumors from X-ray images, 
highlighting a significant advancement in radiology4. While these findings are encouraging, integrating existing 
research into clinical diagnostic workflows still remains a significant challenge5, due to small sample sizes, lim-
ited lesion types, and restricted data sources. Moreover, most existing models overlook several clinical factors 
like age, gender, and the location of bone lesions that play significant roles in the clinical classification of bone 
tumors. One of the reasons for the slow progress in computer-aided diagnosis of primary bone tumors is the 
lack of large, well-annotated, and open-source datasets of bone tumor X-ray images. This deficiency impedes 
the development of new models and raises doubts about the performance of existing models. However, due to 
the low incidence rate of primary bone tumors
6 and widely dispersed patients, it is difficult for researchers to 
access large and diverse medical datasets7. Furthermore, annotating bone tumor imaging data can be extremely 
costly, as it requires the consensus of numerous doctors and radiologists to eliminate biases and human errors8. 
In conclusion, the development of large, well-annotated, open-source bone tumor X-ray image datasets is

the training of AI models. MedPix 9 is a free medical image database managed and maintained by the United 
States (U.S.) National Library of Medicine. It contains thousands of images from various medical fields such as 
radiology, pathology, and dermatology. We identified 1,157 images related to bone tumors in MedPix. However, 
these images are a mix of different imaging types such as X-rays, real images, magnetic resonance imaging 
(MRI), computed tomography (CT) scans, and ultrasound, which prevents us from directly obtaining the spe

beled, making them unsuitable for direct use in deep learning. Radiopaedia.org10 is an open platform dedicated 
to online radiology education which hosts a large collection of X-ray images with detailed clinical information 
and satisfactory quality, many of which are bone tumor X-rays. However, they still lack labels or annotations 
before being used for deep learning. IIEST
11 is an X-ray dataset containing 217 images, but only 69 of which are 
scans of bone tumors. This useful dataset is still insufficient for training deep learning models constrained by its 
small size.
Currently, bone tumor studies based on X-ray images, including classification, localization, and segmenta
-
tion, all use private datasets12–24. Yu He et al.12 obtained 2,899 X-ray images of bone tumors from five medical 
institutions, of which 2,158 were non-malignant and 741 were malignant. However, these images lack clinical 
information such as gender, age, and location. Von Schacky’s CNUH dataset
13 includes 1,195 tumor images 
and 381 normal images. Although this is a large dataset, it only covers knee bone tumors. Claudio E et al .14 
collected 1,045 bone tumor X-ray images (743 benign images and 302 malignant images), without any normal 
bone images. Renyi Liu et al.
15 collected 982 bone tumor X-ray images, demographic information (age, gender, 
location), and other clinical information from 643 patients. However, this dataset comes from a single center 
and does not include normal bone X-ray images. Recently, Jingjing Shao et al.
16 created a dataset by collecting 
X-ray images and clinical information from 333 patients with knee joint giant cell tumors or osteolytic osteo -
sarcomas from four centers. Florian et al.17 gathered 1,792 X-ray images of bone tumors from a single center, 
which provided the anatomical location of the lesions. In addition to the datasets mentioned above, there are 
several other private datasets (e.g., datasets established by Dan Yu
18, Park CW19, and SARAH20). Although each 
of these datasets has unique characteristics, none are publicly available. Therefore, it is difficult for researchers to 
obtain and verify this kind of data, which hinders the development of machine-learning models for bone tumor 
diagnosis. Table 1 summarizes the existing datasets of bone tumor X-ray images and compares them with the 
BTXRD dataset.
In summary, the bone tumor X-ray images available in the existing public datasets are limited and cannot be 
used directly, while private datasets are not publicly accessible. This impedes the development of computer-aided 
diagnosis of primary bone tumors. To address this research gap, we developed an X-ray image dataset named 
BTXRD (accessible at figshare.com [https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.27865398]). The BTXRD was designed 
to facilitate research on the automatic classification, localization, and segmentation of primary bone tumors 
in radiographs. This dataset includes 3,746 images, with 1,879 normal bone images, 1,525 benign bone tumor 
images, and 342 malignant bone tumor images. Each image in the BTXRD dataset is labeled with gender, age, 
the part of the body imaged, and the shooting angle. Specifically, each bone tumor image is additionally tagged 
with benign or malignant labels, subtype labels, and anatomical site labels. Furthermore, each bone tumor image

Methods
ethical clearance.  This retrospective study was approved by the Research Ethics Board of the Medical 
College at Guangxi University (GULL240522). Due to the study’s retrospective nature and the non-invasive meth-
ods used, as well as the anonymity of the data, the informed consent requirement was waived. The study complies 
with the image usage terms of MedPix9 and Radiopaedia.org10.
Data sources. The BTXRD dataset’s images were sourced from three medical image storage centers. Center 
1 includes three hospitals in China: the First Affiliated Hospital of Guangxi Medical University, the Affiliated 
Hospital of Y oujiang Medical College for Nationalities, and Baise People’s Hospital. Center 2 is the Radiopaedia.
org online platform, and Center 3 is the MedPix public database. The BTXRD dataset collected X-ray images 
of patients with primary bone tumors from Center 1, which were confirmed histologically or by two radiology 
experts, from July 2013 to July 2023. Additionally, two radiology experts collected X-ray images of bone tumors 
from Centers 2 and 3 by searching the following keywords: ‘bone tumor, ’ ‘osteochondroma, ’ ‘osteosarcoma, ’ ‘bone 
cyst, ’ ‘fibrous dysplasia, ’ ‘chondroblastoma, ’ ‘fibroma of bone, ’ ‘osteoma, ’ ‘hemangioma, ’ ‘aneurysmal bone cyst, ’ 
‘osteolipoma, ’ ‘giant cell tumor of bone, ’ ‘osteoblastoma, ’ ‘chondrosarcoma, ’ ‘Ewing’s sarcoma, ’ ‘undifferentiated 
pleomorphic sarcoma, ’ ‘fibrosarcoma, ’ ‘angiosarcoma, ’ and ‘epithelioid hemangioendothelioma’ . Only images that 
include corresponding gender and age information were included in the dataset. Each image in the dataset is 
unique, but some may come from the same lesion, as we collected images from different shooting angles (frontal, 
lateral, and oblique views) for each lesion. The workflow of this study is illustrated in Fig. 1, with the main steps, 
including data collection, data cleaning, and data annotation.
Data collection and preprocessing. X-ray images obtained from Center 1 were downloaded in the Digital 
Imaging and Communications in Medicine (DICOM) format from the Picture Archiving and Communication 
System (PACS, GE Healthcare). These images were then converted from DICOM to 8-bit grayscale JPEG (Joint 
Photographic Experts Group) format. Images from Centers 2 and 3 were directly downloaded in JPEG format 
from their official websites, respectively (accessed February 20–28, 2024). In the collected data, we removed pri-
mary bone tumor samples from the head, neck, chest, and abdominal regions since their amount was limited and

are from Center 3. The general distribution characteristics of the BTXRD dataset are listed in Table 2.

Distribution of tumor subtypes and anatomical locations. We analyzed the anatomical locations of 
bone tumors in the BTXRD dataset, with the most common sites being the tibia (630 lesions), femur (592 lesions), 
fibula (259 lesions), and humerus (235 lesions). The detailed distribution of bone tumor anatomical locations is 
shown in Fig. 4. Additionally, we cataloged the subtypes of bone tumors present in the BTXRD dataset images. 
The most frequent subtypes were osteochondroma (754 lesions), osteosarcoma (297 lesions), multiple osteochon-
dromatosis (263 lesions), and simple bone cyst (206 lesions). The detailed distribution of bone tumor subtypes is 
illustrated in Fig. 5.
Data labeling. In the data labeling process, a bone tumor specialist with 10 years of diagnostic experience 
used the Labelme25 image annotation tool to segment tumors and outline them with bounding boxes. This initial 
annotation was subsequently reviewed by another specialist with 20 years of experience. To evaluate the reliability 
of the annotations, 50 bone tumor images were randomly selected from the dataset and re-segmented with bound-
ing boxes reapplied one month after the initial work. The Dice score for the two segmentations was recorded at 
0.90 ± 0.07, and the Intersection over Union (IoU) value for the bounding box placements was 0.91 ± 0.06, indi-
cating high consistency between the two sets of annotations. Labelme is an open-source image annotation tool

Data Records
The BTXRD dataset is openly accessible for public use and available on figshare.com (https://doi.org/10.6084/
m9.figshare.27865398) 26. The total size of BTXRD dataset is 801 MB, which can be downloaded without any 
registration. The file downloaded is in zip format, which includes subfolders “images” and “Annotations,” 
and a “dataset.csv” file. The “images” folder contains all X-ray images, with each image name starting with 
“IMG” followed by a unique zero-padded six-digit value, and ending with “.jpeg” to indicate the data type. 
The “Annotations” folder contains “.json” format files with annotation information for segmentation and 
bounding box placement for each bone tumor image. The “dataset.csv” file contains a summary of the case 
data for all 3,746 images. Table 3 lists an example row from the summary file.
In creating the BTXRD dataset, researchers carefully selected valuable data from a large volume of mixed 
and potentially confounding sources, particularly from Centers 2 and 3. The raw data underwent rigorous pre-
processing, cleaning and selection to exclude incomplete, low-quality, or redundant images, ensuring that only 
high-quality data were retained. Clinical and demographic metadata were standardized into a common format 
to ensure consistency across different sources, facilitating downstream analysis. Additionally, each bone tumor 
image was manually annotated by expert radiologists to generate segmentation masks and bounding boxes, 
using a multi-step review process to minimize biases and improve annotation accuracy. These comprehensive 
efforts have significantly enhanced the value and usability of the BTXRD dataset, providing a valuable resource 
for developing and validating deep learning models for bone tumor diagnosis.

Technical Validation
To examine the suitability of the BTXRD dataset for training deep learning algorithms, we further conducted 
experiments using YOLOv827, a well-established deep learning network commonly used in medical image detec-
tion. The images from the BTXRD dataset were randomly divided into training and validation sets at an 8:2 ratio. 
Annotation information for bone tumor image segmentation and bounding box placement was converted from 
‘ .json’ to ‘ .txt’ format suitable for use with YOLOv8. We utilized YOLOv8s for localization, YOLOv8s-seg for seg
-
mentation, and YOLOv8s-cls for benign-malignant classification of primary bone tumors on the autodl cloud 
platform (www.autodl.com, Zhongke Shituo (Nanjing) Technology Co., Ltd.). The algorithms are implemented 
using Python 3.8.10, PyTorch 2.0.0 + cu118, and the training process is conducted on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 
4090 with 24GB VRAM, and a 16 vCPU Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold 6430 processor. The models were trained using 
standard hyperparameters and official pre-trained weights. The image input size was set to 600 pixels, and the 
models were run for 300 training epochs. Table 4 lists the relative performance for different tasks. The experi
-
mental results demonstrate that the BTXRD dataset is well-suited for application in deep learning algorithms.
Usage Notes
The BTXRD dataset is widely applicable and can be used for various downstream tasks, including validat -
ing existing models or developing new ones. Each image in the BTXRD dataset contains information on age, 
gender, and bone lesion location covering the most common subtypes of primary bone tumors, making it a 
valuable resource for developing computer-assisted subtype-specific or cross-subtype diagnostic models for 
primary bone tumors. The significance of this dataset is three-fold: First, it included the well-known clini
-
cal importance of age, gender, and the location of bone lesions in the diagnosis of bone tumors. Such clinical 
information can help clinicians with accurate and comprehensive diagnoses and is crucial for the development 
of future computer-assisted diagnostic models. Second, most existing models can only be used to perform 
benign-malignant classification tasks, which is insufficient for clinical applications. Classification of bone tumor 
subtypes is more clinically relevant. Thus, future development of computer-assisted diagnostic models should 
focus on the models that can comprehensively classify and diagnose bone tumors. Lastly, the comprehensive 
annotations in the BTXRD dataset are extremely beneficial for developing future reference and new deep learn
-
ing models.
During the construction of the BTXRD dataset, we have devoted substantial manpower and methodical 
effort to identify and annotate bone tumors. However, the quality of the dataset can be further improved from 
the following perspectives. First, due to the lack of pathological information, some images were diagnosed 
empirically by two radiology experts, which could potentially result in misdiagnosis. Second, the morphology 
of some bone tumors on images is very distorted, especially multiple osteochondromas, which may reduce 
the accuracy of segment annotation. Nevertheless, we consider these limitations acceptable, as improving the 
quality of the dataset is an ongoing effort, and we will keep reviewing and refining it. Moving forward, we plan 
to expand multi-center collaboration partnerships to substantially increase the scale of the dataset and engage 
more experts to review the data, thereby reducing potential diagnostic biases and data errors. Additionally, we 
also aim to broaden our dataset to include other types of data modalities, including the CT, MRI, and patho
-
logical data, and in doing so, we hope to establish and make available a comprehensive multimodal resource to 
support interdisciplinary, multidimensional research.
