Aims 
We aim to develop a fully automated deep-learning model to detect and classify benign/ 
malignan
t bone tumours in full-field limb radiographs using an object detection model. 
The secondary aim is to identify differences in classification characteristics between the 
developed automated model, three orthopaedic oncologists, and three general orthopaedic 
surgeons. 
Methods 
This retrospective analysis included 642 limb bone tumours with 40 diagnoses confirmed 
pathologically fr
om three institutions (378 benign, 264 malignant including intermedi
ate types). We employed end-to-end object Detection with transformers with Improved 
deNoising anchOr boxes (DINO) and You Only Look Once (YOLO) models. We performed 
five-fold cross validation on the collected radiographs, using the training data to train the 
models, validation data to optimize the models’ parameters, and independent test data 
for final performance evaluation. Firstly, we confirmed DINO achieves a higher detection 
rate than YOLO. Secondly, we compared the classification performance of DINO with those 
of doctors, using various metrics such as accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, precision, and 
F-measure. 
-
Results 
The DINO model achieved a higher mean tumour detection rate (85.7% (95% CI 81.5 to 
89.8)) than the YOL
O model (80.1% (95% CI 77.2 to 82.9)). For the evaluation of classification 
performance, we used 113 cases that DINO detected out of 128 randomly selected cases as 
the evaluation test set. The accuracy and sensitivity of the DINO model, as a superior model, 
were significantly higher than those of general orthopaedic surgeons. The DINO model 
correctly classified 78.6% (22 out of 28 cases) of the challenging cases that two or more 
doctors misclassified. However, DINO’s diagnostic errors primarily occurred with tumours 
that were diagnostically challenging for orthopaedic oncologists or present in unusual sites. 
760 
Conclusion 
The DINO model automatically detects bone tumours better than the YOLO model, and may assist doctors in detecting tumours 
and classifying malignant/benign bone tumours in clinical practice. 
Article focus 
• Developing a deep learning model to detect and classify 
bone tumours in limb radiographs automatically. 
• Comparing transformer-based and convolutional neural 
network (CNN)-based object detection models in bone 
tumour detection. 
• Evaluating the model’s diagnostic accuracy through 
comparison with three orthopaedic oncologists and three 
general orthopaedic surgeons. 
Key messages 
• The transformer-based Detection with transformers with 
Improved deNoising anchOr boxes (DINO) model demon
strated superior performance over the CNN-based You Only 
Look Once (YOLO) model in bone tumour detection on 
limb radiographs. 
• The model achieved diagnostic accuracy exceeding all 
general orthopaedic surgeons and some orthopaedic 
oncologists in tumour classification. 
• This AI system has demonstrated potential as a clinical 
decision support tool to reduce misdiagnosis of bone 
malignancies. 
-
Strengths and limitations 
• The study demonstrated the superiority of the transformer
based DINO model in bone tumour detection and classifi
cation. 
• This study validated the results using state-of-the-art 
object detection models on the same bone tumour dataset. 
Furthermore, the proposed method demonstrated a 
statistically significant superiority compared to the other 
approach. 
• The study was limited by its relatively small sample size of 
642 cases and the exclusion of non-tumour conditions such 
as degenerative diseases.

Introduction 
Early and pr
ecise identification of bone tumours markedly 
impacts the prognostic outcome. Osteosarcoma, the most 
common primary malignant bone tumour, has a five-year 
survival rate of 60% to 80% in patients without pulmonary 
metastasis at initial diagnosis.
1–3 Although osteosarcoma and 
Ewing’s sarcoma are treated with chemotherapy, 4  surgical 
resection is essential for the curative treatment of malignant 
bone tumours.
5 The prognosis of patients who are difficult to 
resect is inferior.6 
Orthopaedic surgeons must not overlook a malignant 
tumour in a radiograph. Radiography is initially performed 
as a simple screening test, and can provide information on 
the presence or absence of tumours and the classification 
of benign or malignant.
7,8 However, it is difficult for general 
orthopaedic surgeons to classify bone tumours because there 
are many types,
9 and primary malignant bone tumours are 
rare, accounting for only 0.2% of all tumours. 10–12 In addition, 
the rarity and diversity of these make it difficult for general 
orthopaedic surgeons to gain sufficient experience. 
Automated bone tumour diagnostic models using 
AI have been developed to classify benign and malignant 
bone tumours using radiographs.
13–16 In particular, several 
papers have reported that their methods outperformed the 
accuracy of radiologists, general orthopaedic surgeons, and 
orthopaedic oncologists.
13,14 However, in many studies on 
deep learning models for bone tumours, the images used 
were limited to specific radiographs (e.g. only knee or hip) 
or manually cropped images of the tumours.
13,14,16,17 To solve 
these limitations, AI-based object detection models, such as 
Detectron2 and You Only Look Once (YOLO) models, were 
used for automatically detecting and classifying bone tumours 
on full-field radiographs of various body parts.
18,19 The YOLO 
model was promising because of its higher detection accuracy 
than other models such as Detectron2. However, even the 
YOLO model did not perform well in classifying malignant 
tumours. This performance limitation arises from its model 
architecture; specifically, YOLO is based on a convolutional 
neural network (CNN), which is a popular AI model for image 
classification and is also widely used for medical images.
20–26 
CNN extracts local features and aggregates them to make 
its classification or detection without evaluating the relation
ship among distant local parts.
27 In contrast, the bone tumour 
detection and classification task requires more contextual (i.e. 
non-local) information, which is available by observing and 
evaluating the relationship among distant parts in full-field 
radiograph images. 
-
To overcome these limitations, we propose a new 
automatic bone tumour det
ection and classification model. 
The model is based on end-to-end object Detection with 
transformers with Improved deNoising anchOr boxes (DINO),
28 
which is a state-of-the-art object detection model. DINO 
employs a Transformer architecture. Unlike CNN, the Trans
former has an attention mechanism that evaluates the 
relationship among distant image parts,
27,29 and may be able 
to learn whole bone shapes, such as those in limb radiographs. 
Since tumour locations and types are often dependent on 
bone types and their whole shapes, the Transformer architec
ture is expected to be useful in detecting a bone tumour and 
classifying whether the bone tumour is malignant or benign. 
-
-
In this paper, using radiographs of patients with various 
bone tumours from three institutions with bone tumour 
departments, we first show that our DINO-based model can 
detect bone tumours more accurately than YOLO. We then 
show that our model can classify bone tumours with accu
racy that is comparable to or even higher than orthopaedic 
oncologists or general orthopaedic surgeons. These positive 
results prove that both tumour detection and classification 
tasks need wider views to capture non-local relationships in 
each full-field radiograph image.

Methods 
Dataset 
The Institutional Review Board of Kyushu University Hospital 
(No.22316-00, No.22089-00) approved this study. Cases with 
a radiograph of the limb with a visible bone tumour at the 
initial visit and with a definitive pathological diagnosis were 
included in our dataset (cases with multiple bone tumours 
were excluded). The cases were collected from three tertiary 
referral centres that have departments specializing in bone 
tumour treatment because these cases included a wide range 
of bone tumours, including both common and rare types. The 
patient population encompasses various age groups, tumour 
locations, and disease severities, ensuring the representative-
ness of the general population of bone tumour patients 
requiring treatment in Japan. Table I shows the details of 
the dataset. We divided this dataset into five subsets for 
five-fold cross-validation in the later experiment. This table 
also details the evaluation test set; we compare the classifica
tion performance of an object detection model with those of 
orthopaedic oncologists and general orthopaedic surgeons.

Object detec
tion models and training procedure 
Figure 1 illustrates this method’s overview. As preprocessing, 
the input images were resized to 512 by 512 pixels, and the 
left-side images were flipped to the right, except for the hip 
images. The images were then fed into the object detec
tion models. The models automatically detected tumours 
as bounding boxes and classified them as benign or malig
nant. The DINO model
28 and the YOLO-v8x model 31  were 
used to detect and classify tumours because the models are 
object detection models with excellent performance. Both 
DINO and YOLO models are open-source frameworks that 
are freely available for implementation via GitHub. These 
models were selected to address two clinical challenges: the 
difficulty orthopaedic surgeons face in detecting subtle bone 
tumours on radiographs, and the limited experience many 
general orthopaedic surgeons have in differentiating between 
benign and malignant tumours. The DINO model is based 
on the so-called Transformer,
29 which employs the atten
tion mechanism. Roughly speaking, the attention mechanism 
can calculate (adaptively weighted) spatial correlation over 
the whole input image and thus captures its global image 
features.
32 Consequently, the DINO model has a potential of 
recognizing age-related bone structure variations and tumour 
predilection sites by analyzing relationships between local 
tumour features and distant anatomical landmarks. In contrast, 
the YOLO model is based on a CNN, which employs layered 
convolution operations to aggregate local information and 
therefore is suitable for extracting local features.
27 “v8x” is the 
recent version of YOLO models used in related studies and 
other medical field studies.
19,33–35 By training these models on 
full-field radiographs, we developed systems that align with 
clinical practice to assist doctors in making faster and more 
accurate diagnostic decisions. For training and evaluating 
these object detection models, we implemented a five-fold 
cross-validation. The 642 cases in the dataset were randomly 
divided into five subsets (Fold0-Fold4), ensuring no overlap 
across the different subsets. Next, each subset was used as 
a test dataset, and the cases in the other four subsets were 
randomly divided into validation (20%) and training (80%) 
sets. Each test dataset included only radiographs obtained at 
the initial visit. For each fold of five-fold cross-validation, we 
trained the models using training data, optimized the models’ 
hyper-parameters (especially, training epochs, that is, the 
timing to stop training) using validation data, and performed 
the final evaluation using test data. Through this cross-vali
dation process, performance results were obtained from five 
different model evaluations. The details of the number of 
cases and images used are shown in Supplementary Figure 
a. We used data augmentation (cropping and rotation) during 
training to compensate for the limited data and variations 
in bone positioning and angular differences during radiolog
ical imaging. Examples of image rotation and cropping for 
data augmentation are shown in Supplementary Figure b. For 
technical details on object detection models, see Supplemen
tary Methods.

Evaluation methods of differences in performance between 
DINO and YOLO 
The detection performance of the DINO model was com
pared with that of the YOLO-v8x to confirm its superiority 
to the YOLO-v8x. We used several metrics to evaluate the 
tumour detection performance: overall detection rate, those 
for benign and malignant tumours, and mean Intersection 
over Union (IoU). For each input image, the models output 
candidate bounding boxes, their classes (benign or malig
nant), and their corresponding confidence scores. We selected 
the bounding box with the highest confidence score as the 
final estimation result of the models. The detection rate was 
then calculated as the percentage of cases that were success
fully detected in the test dataset. We considered the detec
tion successful if the IoU exceeded a threshold of 0.1. This 
threshold was used because a 10% overlap with the actual 
region is sufficient for orthopaedic surgeons to localize the 
proper area.

Evaluation methods of differences in performance bet
ween 
DINO and doctors 
To quantify the clinical efficacy of the DINO model, we 
compared its tumour classification performance with those of 
three experienced orthopaedic oncologists (Dr. SA, Dr. SB, and 
Dr. SC; 17, 12, and 18 years of experience, respectively) and 
three general orthopaedic surgeons (Dr. GA, Dr.GB, and Dr. GC; 
10 years of experience). 
We used several metrics to evaluate tumour classifica
tion performance: accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, precision, 
and F-measure, which were calculated using scikit-learn.

Note that these metrics were assessed using the evaluation 
test set, which included the successful detection cases of the 
superior model’s Fold1-test set. This set was used because the 
superior model achieved a slightly better tumour detection

Discussion 
In this study
, we first developed deep learning models 
for bone tumour det
ection and classification of benign 
and malignant tumours using the Transformer-based object 
detection models. This is the first study to establish 
the Transformer-based model to detect and classify malig
nant/benign bone tumours and to compare the accuracy of 
different architecture-based object detection models. Due to 
the Transformer’s ability to extract features by considering 
the relationships across the entire image, the DINO model 
had significantly higher detection rates than the YOLO-v8x 
model (85.7% vs 80.1%; p = 0.014). The DINO model obvi
ously showed higher detection rates, especially for malignant 
tumours (89.0% vs 81.1%). 
-
-
Notably, the DINO model’s classification accuracy 
score surpassed those of all general orthopaedic surgeons. 
Misdiagnosis of malignant tumours is disadvantageous to 
patients, but the DINO model showed a significantly higher 
sensitivity than that of one orthopaedic oncologist and three

Few related studies have reported on deep-learning 
models for the fully automated detection and classification 
of benign and malignant bone tumours using full-field bone 
radiographs of various body parts.
18,19 Although the data

model was 84.3%, indicating its high capability for classify
ing malignant tumours. Although bone tumours are rare, our

sufficient for conducting a meaningful analysis.

This study has some limitations. First, although this 
was unavoidable considering the rarity of bone tumours, 
642 cases, the number of cases studied, needed to be 
Bone & Joint Research Volume 14, No. 9 September 2025 766 
sufficiently large compared to studies on common cancers. 
Second, the input image size was limited to 512 × 512 
pixels due to Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) memory capacity 
constraints. Future studies will require increased GPU memory 
capacity to process higher-resolution images. Third, the cases 
studied were limited to instances of limbs and did not include 
vertebrae, pelvis, or ribs. Evaluating these trunk bones is 
expected to be difficult using radiography alone, so additional 
studies using other imaging methods are needed. Fourth, 
the current model cannot detect and classify targets other 
than bone tumours. The models were not trained on images 
without tumour lesions or with fractures or degenerative 
diseases. Future studies should include those images to make 
clinical applications more feasible. Furthermore, it could be 
a future option to take into account clinical findings such as 
pain, sex, age and blood test results, in addition to imaging to 
classify malignant/benign bone tumours more precisely. 
In summary, the Transformer-based DINO model 
automatically detects bone tumours in full-field limb 
radiographs more accurately than the CNN-based YOLO 
model. This model could serve as a valuable support tool in 
daily clinical practice, particularly assisting general orthopae
dic surgeons who may be less familiar with bone tumours. 
While radiological review by doctors remains essential, 
especially for complex cases, the model’s high-accuracy 
diagnostic capabilities could help reduce doctor workload 
through automated tumour detection and classification, 
particularly in avoiding misdiagnosing malignant tumours as 
benign ones. Future studies are warranted to make clinical 
applications more feasible.
